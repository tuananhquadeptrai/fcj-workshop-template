# Summary Report: "First Cloud Journey Meetup 30/05/2026"

### Event Overview

The **First Cloud Journey Meetup 30/05/2026** was a community knowledge-sharing event with topics across AWS learning, cloud architecture, hackathon experience, DevOps, and personal development. The materials shared in the Google Drive folder show that the meetup was not only about technical skills, but also about learning mindset, overcoming procrastination, building confidence, and learning through real community experiences.

### Event Objectives

- Share practical AWS learning paths using AWS Cloud Quest and local emulation tools.
- Help participants recognize learning barriers such as fear, self-doubt, and procrastination.
- Encourage students and junior builders to join hackathons and learn by building real prototypes.
- Introduce cloud architecture, DevOps thinking, and ways to prevent project problems before they become incidents.
- Connect FCJ community members through practical presentations and shared experiences.

### Sessions and Main Topics

#### Level Up Your AWS Skills with Cloud Quest and Floci

Speaker **Huynh Thai Linh** presented how beginners can learn AWS hands-on more effectively and with less risk. The session began with common concerns when practicing AWS, such as checking the bill too often, forgetting to delete resources, and worrying about unexpected cloud charges.

Key points:

- **AWS Cloud Quest** was introduced as a free guided AWS practice environment built around game-like quests, making it easier for beginners to learn by doing.
- **Floci** is an open source tool for local AWS service emulation, helping learners test architecture quickly without cloud cost.
- Floci was compared with LocalStack Community in areas such as performance, memory and storage usage, service support, compatibility, licensing, and pricing.
- The speaker also noted that Floci cannot fully replace real AWS testing because its service support is limited and some results are mock-based.

The practical learning roadmap was to use **Cloud Quest** for mindset and architecture, **Floci** for code and fast testing, and **real AWS** for deployment and production validation.

#### The Iceberg of Procrastination

Speaker **Khac Uy Pham** shared the topic **"The Iceberg of Procrastination: When Laziness Is Just the Tip of Fear"**. The presentation explained that procrastination is not simply laziness. Laziness is only the visible tip of the iceberg, while the deeper cause is often fear.

Three layers of fear were highlighted:

- **Fear of not being good enough:** seeing a roadmap with Kubernetes, Terraform, and microservices, then feeling overwhelmed.
- **Fear of judgment:** writing code or having an idea but not daring to push it to GitHub or share it.
- **Fear of failure:** using procrastination as a way to avoid the possibility of trying hard and still failing.

The speaker suggested two practical actions:

- **Name the fear:** instead of saying "I am lazy", identify what you are actually afraid of.
- **Use the 5-minute rule:** do not try to finish everything at once; just start with a small action such as opening the laptop, writing a few lines of code, or reading one AWS documentation page.

The main message was that **action creates confidence**, not the other way around.

#### Why We Always Need Confidence

Speaker **Nguyen Thi Quynh Nhu** discussed why confidence matters in student life, careers, communication, and everything in between. The session redefined confidence: it is not ego, perfection, or always having the right answer. Confidence means trying even when we still feel nervous.

Important ideas:

- Self-doubt can lead to missed chances, hidden potential, and pressure that other people cannot see.
- A person may understand the technical content well but still fail to communicate, present, or defend an idea without confidence.
- Concepts such as **imposter syndrome** and the **Dunning-Kruger effect** show that self-doubt is common during the learning journey.
- Confidence helps people connect, collaborate, take ownership, make decisions, and learn from mistakes.

The speaker recommended building everyday confidence through preparation, celebrating small wins, using the 5-second rule, and acting before self-doubt pulls us back.

#### How Cloud Architecture Powers Modern Multiplayer Games

The topic by **Pham Quang Thai** in the Drive folder was **"How Cloud Architecture Powers Modern Multiplayer Games"**. Since the Drive material only showed the topic name, this report records it at a high level: modern multiplayer games need cloud architecture to handle player connections, scale resources with traffic, reduce latency, and maintain a stable gameplay experience.

From this topic, I learned that online games do not depend only on client-side gameplay. They also require backend systems that can scale, support real-time communication, store state properly, and monitor service health when many players connect at the same time.

#### Hackathon - More Like "HA! A Tons, of Fun"

The team **The Ballers** shared their hackathon experience. The presentation defined a hackathon as a fast-paced event, often lasting 24 to 48 hours, where teams build a prototype or MVP to solve a real-world problem. Hackathons encourage collaboration, creativity, rapid iteration, and learning by doing.

Benefits of joining a hackathon:

- Turning theory into practice through a real project.
- Creating a product or prototype for a portfolio, CV, or GitHub.
- Meeting developers, designers, mentors, recruiters, and other teams.
- Practicing brainstorming, teamwork, pressure handling, and pitching.

The team shared their 36-hour experience with two projects:

- **SynthHunter:** an AI voice verification system that classifies audio as human, AI-generated, or needs review, using approaches such as speech dynamics, encoder behavior, and temporal rhythm.
- **Vortex:** a platform that simulates the hiring journey from CV screening to behavioral and technical interviews, helping candidates prepare for unknown situations.

The results mentioned in the slides were **Top 21/Top 25 overall** and **Top 10 AWS Track**. The biggest lessons were to start from a real problem, stay persistent, experiment constantly, use available tools well, and not be afraid to join even as a first-timer.

#### The Hidden Iceberg of a Project: DevOps Before Disaster

Speaker **Tran Minh Quan** presented DevOps through the iceberg model. A "simple feature" may be estimated at two days, but in reality it can lead to rework, misunderstandings, bugs, failed deployments, and team exhaustion. Visible symptoms such as missed deadlines, production bugs, failed deployments, customer complaints, and burnout are only the tip of the iceberg.

Hidden causes included:

- Ambiguous requirements.
- Communication gaps between teams.
- Siloed teams.
- Lack of ownership.
- Manual processes.
- Slow feedback loops.

The speaker emphasized that DevOps is not only about Docker, Kubernetes, CI/CD, Cloud, or Terraform. DevOps is a foundation built on **people, process, and technology**. Its core principles include collaboration to break silos, automation to reduce human error, fast feedback to catch problems earlier, and continuous improvement to avoid repeating failures.

### Key Takeaways

#### Technical Knowledge

- AWS learning should begin with guided environments such as Cloud Quest, then use local tools such as Floci for faster practice and lower cost risk.
- Cloud architecture is important for systems that need scalability, real-time communication, or many concurrent users.
- Hackathons are a strong environment for learning how to turn ideas into products under time pressure.
- DevOps helps teams look beneath visible symptoms and address root causes before disasters happen.

#### Soft Skills and Growth Mindset

- Procrastination often comes from fear, so it is important to identify emotions and begin with small actions.
- Confidence is built through preparation, experience, small wins, and acting even while nervous.
- A successful project requires technical skills, communication, ownership, learning from mistakes, and teamwork.

### Applying to My Internship

After joining this meetup, I can apply several lessons to my internship:

- Be more careful when practicing AWS by controlling resources and costs.
- Use local or sandbox environments before deploying to real cloud services.
- When facing a difficult task, break it into smaller steps and start instead of waiting for perfect confidence.
- Improve presentation skills, progress sharing, and the ability to defend ideas in a team.
- Look at project issues through a DevOps mindset: find root causes, improve the process, and shorten feedback loops.
